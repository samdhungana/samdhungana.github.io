I think Garmin is a better business than the market is giving it credit for, and the market keeps anchoring on the wrong comparison. Every time Garmin comes up, the conversation defaults to smartwatch unit share against Apple and Samsung, where Garmin is a rounding error at roughly 4%. Garmin doesn't compete for the mass smartwatch buyer, it competes for the runner, cyclist, sailor, hiker, and pilot who will pay a real premium for battery life and GPS reliability that Apple still doesn't match, and it has been quietly compounding through that niche for two straight years of 30%+ fitness segment growth while trading like a mature, low-growth hardware company. I built a DCF off Garmin's five reporting segments, Fitness, Outdoor, Aviation, Marine, and Auto OEM, blended into a single company-wide growth rate, plus a comps cross-check off Garmin's own reported financials, and both tell the same story: Garmin is a good business, run conservatively, priced for far less growth than it's already delivering.

The Business

Garmin has been building GPS-enabled hardware for more than 35 years across five segments: Fitness, Outdoor, Aviation, Marine, and Auto OEM. It has shipped over 300 million products since inception, more than 20 million of them in fiscal 2025 alone. In Q1 2026 (reported April 29, 2026), the company posted record first-quarter revenue of $1.75 billion, up 14% year over year, with operating income up 30% and pro forma EPS of $2.08, beating consensus by roughly 15%.

What makes Garmin a good business rather than just a good quarter is the combination of a real product moat and a balance sheet that lets it keep investing through any part of the cycle. Garmin products like the Fenix 8 and Enduro 3 offer 48 to 320 hours of GPS battery life depending on configuration, versus roughly 14 hours for the Apple Watch Ultra 3. That gap doesn't matter to a casual walker, but it is the entire product for an ultramarathoner, a multi-day hiker, or an Ironman athlete, and those are exactly the customers with the highest willingness to pay and the least price sensitivity. On top of that, Garmin runs vertically integrated manufacturing across Taiwan, the U.S., the Netherlands, the U.K., Poland, and China, which gives it tighter quality control than a company that outsources everything, and a training-and-race-history dataset inside Garmin Connect that raises switching costs the longer a customer stays in the ecosystem. None of these three things alone is a fortress. Together, they explain why Garmin has kept growing fitness revenue more than 30% a year while carrying 58.7% gross margins and zero long-term debt, and why I don't think that combination gets replicated quickly by a competitor chasing the category.

The other piece that gets underweighted is how diversified the earnings base already is. Aviation, Marine, and Outdoor together are roughly 45% of revenue and behave nothing like consumer electronics. Garmin has ranked first in pilot support satisfaction for 21 consecutive years, and once a G1000 avionics system is certified into an aircraft, an operator is not ripping it out over a product refresh cycle. Aviation grew 18% in Q1 2026 at 75% gross margins. That's the kind of segment that makes a five-segment hardware company look a lot more like a compounder than a bet on a single fad, and it's a big part of why I'm comfortable underwriting the fitness growth without assuming the whole company falls apart if running slows down for a year.

Why This Is Mispriced

I believe this isn't a hidden-value situation, since Garmin is covered by seven sell-side analysts. What I think is actually happening is a framing problem. The average rating across that coverage is a Hold (1.85), and even the bulls tend to talk about Garmin using smartwatch-market language: unit share, ASPs versus Apple, replacement-cycle risk. Once you frame Garmin as a niche hardware compounder with a growing services option (Garmin Connect+, Connect IQ, and the MYLAPS acquisition) rather than a smartwatch also-ran, the growth Garmin has already delivered, 36.8% fitness growth in FY2024, 32.9% in FY2025, 42% in Q1 2026, looks a lot less like a COVID sugar high and a lot more like a durable participation trend the market hasn't fully repriced. US marathon finishers hit 432,562 in 2024, up 5% year over year and a fourth straight year above pre-COVID levels. Strava now has 135 million users, running clubs on the platform grew 59% in 2024, and Garmin was the second most-used device across all Strava activity in 2025, trailing only Strava's own app.

Management's own guidance pattern reinforces the mispricing. Garmin has beaten consensus EPS in 14 of the last 16 quarters and raised full-year guidance in both FY2024 and FY2025. Coming into FY2024, management guided fitness segment growth to roughly 10% and delivered 36.8%. After beating Q1 2026 revenue by 14%, management held full-year guidance at $7.9 billion revenue and $9.35 pro forma EPS, citing that Q1 is seasonally the smallest quarter, the same conservative posture that preceded the last two guidance raises. I'm not underwriting another beat of that size as my base case, but a management team that guides this conservatively and still beats this consistently is not a management team I'd bet against on the numbers already on the table.

Valuation

I built this off a DCF anchored to Garmin's five reporting segments, Fitness, Outdoor, Aviation, Marine, and Auto OEM, each carrying its own growth assumption off FY2025 actuals and recent trends, blended into a single company-wide growth rate rather than carried through as five separate dollar build-ups. That's a simplification, but the segment-level growth inputs are still the actual driver, not a single top-down guess, and I cross-checked the output against a comps set and sell-side consensus.

Revenue builds from $7.25 billion in FY2025 to $7.69 billion in FY2026E and $10.28 billion by FY2030E, a roughly 7.3% five-year CAGR. I'm holding EBIT margin close to flat around 27% through the forecast rather than assuming further expansion, which is deliberately conservative relative to Garmin's recent trend but consistent with sell-side consensus margin estimates of 26.3% in FY2026E, 25.9% in FY2027E, and 25.7% in FY2028E. On that basis, unlevered free cash flow runs from about $1.83 billion in FY2026E to $2.23 billion by FY2030E, discounted at an 8.4% WACC on a mid-year basis, with a terminal value set off a 17x exit EV/EBITDA multiple against FY2030E EBITDA of roughly $3.06 billion.

For the exit multiple, I used 18x using FactSet data. Garmin already trades at roughly 19x today, and I don't think a full reversion to the peer median is the right assumption given it has the cleanest balance sheet in the group, the best margins, and the most segment diversification, but I also didn't want to simply assume today's multiple holds forever, since that's a circular argument (the market's current multiple, whatever it is, isn't proof that it's the right one). 18x splits the difference and is the most defensible number I can point to without just backing into a target.

That produces a present value of the five-year cash flows plus terminal value of about $44.8 billion in enterprise value, of which the terminal value is roughly 82%, consistent with a long-duration compounder, not a business I'm assuming melts down after the forecast window. Add back $4.3 billion of net cash and divide by 194.3 million fully diluted shares (192.7 million basic plus RSU dilution), and the base case comes out to about $253 a share. I stress-tested this across WACC from 7.4% to 9.4% and exit multiples from 16x to 20x: the grid ranges from roughly $223 at the low end (9.4% WACC, 16x exit) to about $285 at the high end (7.4% WACC, 20x exit), with the current-assumption cell at $253. I'd rather show you the low end of that range than pretend the point estimate is more precise than it is.

The comps cross-check points the same direction, if a bit higher. Garmin trades around 19x LTM EV/EBITDA and 26x LTM P/E against a peer group of Logitech, Trimble, Amer Sports, Curtiss-Wright, Visteon, and Brunswick that sits at a median closer to 17x EV/EBITDA and 25x P/E. I think Garmin deserves to sit at the top of that range, since it has the cleanest balance sheet in the group with $4.3 billion of net cash and no debt, and the best combination of margin and segment momentum. Sell-side consensus (seven broker contributors, per FactSet) puts the average price target at $279.40 against a Hold-leaning average rating of 1.85, which tells me the analysts collectively see more upside in the number than their own ratings reflect, a pattern that shows up often with names the sell-side is structurally cautious on but can't argue down on the model.

Putting the three together, DCF base case of $253, comps support in the high $250s to high $260s, and sell-side consensus at $279.40, I'm setting a target of $265. That's the middle of the range rather than the top, because I'd rather be right using the more conservative DCF and comps as the anchor than borrow the optimism embedded in consensus. From $240 today, $265 is about 10% upside before dividends, over an 18 to 24 month holding period. Annualized, that's roughly a 6% price return on top of Garmin's dividend. That's a modest IRR, and I want to be upfront about that rather than dress it up. The stock has moved up toward fair value since I first ran this, and there's less margin of safety in the DCF alone than there was a few months ago. The case here rests more on comps and consensus convergence, and on the market eventually re-rating Garmin's growth durability, than on the DCF showing a large embedded discount.

The Bear Case

The strongest pushback I've heard on this comes from two places on the sell side: Eric Compton at Morningstar (Hold, $220 target, May 2026) and Baptista Research (Underweight, $246.50 target, May 2026), and I think their argument deserves a real answer rather than a dismissal. The case, as I understand it, is that Garmin is still fundamentally a hardware company, and hardware companies get multiple-compressed over time regardless of near-term growth, because eventually growth normalizes and the market re-rates the stock down to a lower multiple on a lower growth rate. Layered onto that is a specific concern that Garmin's 30%+ fitness growth over the last two years is largely COVID-era replacement demand working through the installed base, and once that wave finishes cycling through, which could be as soon as this year or next, growth could step down sharply, and the market could re-rate Garmin to something closer to a legacy consumer electronics multiple in the low double digits rather than its current ~19x.

I take this seriously because it's directionally correct about the mechanism, Garmin is a hardware company, and hardware multiples do compress when growth slows. Where I disagree is on magnitude and timing. The replacement-cycle argument would be a lot more worrying if the growth were only showing up in unit volumes off a 2020 to 2021 cohort rolling through a fixed three-to-four year cycle. But the participation data, marathon finishers still growing, Strava usage still growing, half marathons now 30% of road race participation versus 17% a decade ago, suggests the demand base is still expanding, not just refreshing. And even in Compton's and Baptista's own more bearish scenarios, their price targets of $220 and $246.50 are still within about 3% to 8% of today's $240, which is a much smaller downside than the multiple-compression story would imply if you took it to its logical extreme. I read that as the bear case capping the downside more than it's calling for a collapse, which is a very different risk profile than a broken thesis.

Catalysts

The nearest one is straightforward: the Q2 2026 print, scheduled for July 29, 2026, where I want to see fitness growth stay in the double digits even against a tougher year-over-year comparison, and gross margin holding near the 58 to 59% range without giving back the Q1 expansion once management said some of that was FX-driven. Further out, Auto OEM is guided down in 2026 as the BMW program rolls off peak volumes, that's already known and priced, but the next program, with Mercedes-Benz, is expected to ramp starting in 2027 with higher-ASP units than BMW. That's the catalyst that could reaccelerate total company growth in 2027 to 2028, and it's the one thing in my own model I can't yet fully corroborate: FactSet consensus has total revenue growth reaccelerating to roughly 14.5% in FY2028, well above the ~8% my own growth assumptions produce, and Auto OEM/Mercedes timing is my best guess for the gap. I'm treating it as upside optionality rather than something I'm underwriting.

Risks

Apple shipping a genuine multi-day-battery endurance watch would be the single biggest threat to the moat, I'd watch for any signal of that at a fall Apple event. Garmin's manufacturing footprint is still concentrated in Taiwan, so a tariff escalation or a China/Taiwan geopolitical shock is a real tail risk to margins. A consumer recession would hit Garmin harder than a staples company given how discretionary its price points are. And there's a normalization risk that the running boom itself cools off faster than the participation data currently suggests, which is really the same risk as the bear case above, just without the wait for a sell-side downgrade to tell me about it.

Bottom Line

A $253 DCF, comps support in the high $250s to $260s, and sell-side consensus at $279.40 all point the same direction. $265 gets me there without needing to believe the most optimistic number in the range, though at $240 today, this is a lower-conviction entry point than it would have been a few months ago, and the upside case leans more on comps and consensus than on the DCF alone.
