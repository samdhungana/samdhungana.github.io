1. Company Overview

For more than 35 years, Garmin Ltd. and its subsidiaries have pioneered new products, many of which feature location technology such as Global Positioning System (GPS), services and applications designed for people who live an active lifestyle. Garmin serves five primary markets: fitness, outdoor, aviation, marine, and auto OEM. Since inception, Garmin has delivered over 300 million products, including more than 20 million delivered during fiscal 2025.

Garmin Connect and Garmin Connect Mobile are web and mobile platforms where users track and analyze their fitness, activities, and workouts. Garmin Connect+ layers premium features, including personalized insights and performance dashboards, on a subscription basis. The Connect IQ developer platform lets third parties build watch faces, apps, widgets, and data fields for Garmin devices.

In Q1 2026, Garmin posted record first-quarter revenue of $1.75 billion, up 14% year over year, with operating income up 30% and pro forma EPS of $2.08, beating consensus by roughly 15%. CEO Cliff Pemble framed the result by saying the company continues to outperform expectations in a business environment characterized by economic whiplash and geopolitical uncertainty, and that even in these conditions, great products and customer service always win.

Opportunities

Threats

  • Running boom normalization
  • Consumer recession pressuring discretionary demand
  • Tariff escalation — US-China-Taiwan tensions
  • Low-cost competitors (Huawei, Xiaomi) moving upmarket

3. Investment Thesis

Garmin is a premium fitness and outdoor technology company that the market still tends to value like a mature hardware business. I believe the stock is attractive because the company is benefiting from a durable increase in running and endurance participation, while its customer base is more loyal and resilient than investors fully appreciate. The fitness segment has significantly outperformed expectations over the past two years, growing 36.8% in FY2024 and 32.9% in FY2025, and carried that momentum into Q1 2026 with 42% growth. Despite this growth, Garmin has continued to generate strong margins, high free cash flow, and maintains a debt-free balance sheet.

Pillar 1: Runflation and Endurance Participation Growth

The strongest driver behind Garmin's fitness business is the continued growth in running and endurance participation. US marathon finishers reached 432,562 in 2024, up 5% year over year and above pre-COVID levels for the fourth straight year. The top 100 US races grew 15% in the second half of 2024. Running participation in the UK increased 39% year over year, while IRONMAN registered more than 200,000 athletes in 2024.

The half marathon now represents 30% of all road race participation, up from 17% a decade ago — newer runners entering longer-distance events are one of the largest customer groups for GPS watches. Strava now has 135 million users globally; running clubs on the platform increased 59% in 2024, and Garmin ranked as the second most-used device across all Strava activities in 2025, behind only the Strava mobile app itself. Garmin's fitness revenue grew 36.8% in FY2024 and 32.9% in FY2025, then accelerated to 42% in Q1 2026 to a first-quarter record of $547 million.

Pillar 2: COVID-Era Upgrade Cycle

Garmin sold a large number of devices during the running and outdoor boom of 2020 and 2021. Many of those users are now reaching the typical three-to-four year replacement cycle. It matters more for Garmin than for lower-end smartwatch companies because Garmin's users are generally more engaged and loyal — someone who has tracked training data, races, sleep metrics, and workouts on Garmin Connect for several years is more likely to remain in the ecosystem when upgrading.

Pillar 3: Durable Moat in Endurance Wearables

Garmin's competitive position is strongest in endurance and outdoor use cases where battery life, GPS reliability, and training features matter more than general smartwatch functionality. The Fenix 8 offers up to 60 hours of GPS battery life, while the Enduro 3 reaches as much as 320 hours with solar charging. The Apple Watch Ultra 3 lasts around 14 hours in full GPS mode.

Garmin also runs vertically integrated manufacturing across Taiwan, the U.S., the Netherlands, the U.K., Poland, and China. Owning its own factories and distribution gives tighter quality control and the flexibility to serve a broad product range, and is a partial answer to the tariff bears, since a multi-country footprint gives more room to adapt than single-country dependence would.

Pillar 4: Diversified Earnings Base and Balance Sheet Strength

Wearables get the most attention, but aviation, marine, and outdoor products together make up roughly 45% of total revenue, and these businesses tend to have more stable demand and higher switching costs than consumer electronics. Garmin has ranked first in pilot support satisfaction for 21 straight years, and its G1000 cockpit system is widely used in general aviation aircraft across North America; aviation grew 18% in Q1 2026 with gross margins of 75%.

Three of the five segments posted double-digit growth in Q1 2026; Marine grew 11% even while absorbing tariff-driven margin pressure, and the only soft spots were Outdoor (a tough comparison against the prior-year Instinct 3 launch) and Auto OEM (a planned transition year). As of Q1 2026, Garmin had approximately $4.3 billion in cash and marketable securities and no long-term debt, with Q1 2026 free cash flow of roughly $469 million against operating income of $432 million.

Pillar 5: Underappreciated Ecosystem and Valuation

Garmin has more than 40 million active devices connected to Garmin Connect, and the MYLAPS acquisition strengthens relationships with race organizers and endurance events. I don't think Garmin should be valued mainly as a software or subscription business today — most of its value still comes from hardware sales and its position in the endurance market. At the same time, the market may be overlooking the long-term value of Garmin's ecosystem: Connect IQ deepens the platform the same way an app store deepens a phone platform, and against a base of 40M+ connected devices, even modest Connect+ subscription attach rates would be meaningful.

There's also a concrete, management-confirmed earnings catalyst the market isn't yet paying for. Auto OEM revenue is expected to decline in 2026 as the BMW program reaches peak volumes and legacy programs roll off — that's known bad news. The next large-scale program, with Mercedes-Benz, is expected to drive significant growth starting in 2027 with higher average selling prices than the BMW program it replaces. On valuation, the stock trades at roughly 25x forward earnings while the fitness segment grew more than 30% in FY2025 and 42% in Q1 2026 — I think the market is still underestimating the durability of Garmin's business and the optionality in its ecosystem and Auto OEM pipeline.

4. Valuation

I triangulated the target using a five-scenario DCF and a comparable companies analysis, and the two methods support each other.

The DCF uses a WACC of 8.4%, a 17x terminal EV/EBITDA exit multiple, and revenue and margin assumptions deliberately anchored to management's own guidance rather than above it. The base case produces an implied share price of about $259, an enterprise value near $46 billion, an implied EV/EBITDA of roughly 21x, and an implied perpetuity growth rate of about 3.4%. Terminal value is around 82% of enterprise value, which is normal for a quality compounder but a reminder that the exit multiple matters, so I stress-tested it — across scenarios, the base, management, and upside cases cluster in the high $250s to mid $330s, while the two downside cases protect against the runflation trend slowing and tariffs biting harder.

The comparable companies analysis cross-checks the result. Garmin trades around 19x LTM EV/EBITDA and 26x LTM P/E, while the peer group sits at a median near 17x EV/EBITDA and 25x P/E. Garmin deserves to trade at the higher end of that range, and arguably above it, because it has the cleanest balance sheet in the group with $4.3 billion of net cash, the strongest segment momentum, and best-in-class margins. Applying peer-supported forward multiples to Garmin's earnings power lands in the high $270s to high $280s.

Taking the DCF base case, the scenario range, and the comps together, I set the target at $285. That is a deliberately defensible number — an earlier version of this work implied a price north of $330, but that required stacking aggressive revenue, margin, and multiple assumptions all at once. A $285 target asks far less of the future and, I think, is a stronger pitch precisely because it does not require assuming I know the business better than the CEO does on revenue or the CFO does on margins.

5. Supporting Factors for a Bullish Case

a. Conservative Guidance History

Garmin management has a history of guiding conservatively and then delivering better results than expected — it has beaten consensus EPS estimates in 14 of the last 16 quarters and raised guidance during both FY2024 and FY2025. In FY2024, management initially guided fitness segment growth at around 10% but ultimately delivered 36.8% growth. After Q1 2026, management held full-year guidance at $7.9 billion revenue and $9.35 pro forma EPS despite a 14% revenue beat, noting the first quarter is seasonally the lowest and much of the year remains ahead. I don't think investors should assume another large beat automatically, but management's track record suggests current expectations may still be conservative relative to underlying demand.

b. Garmin's Position Within the Watch Market

Most smartwatch comparisons focus on unit shipments, but Garmin looks stronger when viewed through revenue, margins, and customer mix — it has only around 4% global smartwatch unit share, yet ranks third in global watch revenue behind Apple and Samsung, competing in a more premium part of the market with customers willing to spend more on specialized products.

c. AI Productivity as a Small Long-Term Tailwind

I don't view AI productivity as a core part of the thesis, but it could become a modest long-term tailwind. Research from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis suggests generative AI users are beginning to save meaningful amounts of time each week, and the same 25-to-45-year-old professional demographic benefiting most from these tools is also heavily represented in running clubs and endurance sports participation data. There's no direct evidence linking AI adoption to Garmin sales, but if knowledge workers gain more flexibility and continue prioritizing fitness and outdoor activities, that could support long-term participation trends over time.

6. Key Metrics to Monitor

These are the numbers I watch each quarter. If they stay healthy, the thesis still works. If they weaken materially, the thesis needs to be revisited.

  • Fitness revenue growth — the most important metric. Above 20% is very strong, 15–20% is still solid, below 10% for two straight quarters could signal slowing demand or share loss. After a 42% Q1, the bar for the rest of 2026 is naturally lower on a reported basis given tougher comparisons, so I'm watching the underlying unit and share trend rather than the headline alone.
  • Gross margins — expanded from 57.5% to 58.7% over the last two years and posted 59.4% in Q1 2026. A sustained move below 57% could point to pricing pressure or weaker product mix; I'm separating real margin gains from FX, since management said the Q1 expansion was primarily FX-driven, and watching for tariff and component cost pressure expected to become more visible in 2027.
  • Strava device share — Garmin was the second most-used device platform on Strava in 2025 behind the Strava mobile app itself. If Garmin falls behind Apple Health, that may suggest Apple is gaining traction with serious runners. Check Strava's annual Year in Sport report each January.
  • Race registration trends — RunSignup and RunRepeat publish race participation data throughout the year. Two quarters of declining registrations could suggest demand is cooling faster than expected.
  • Apple Watch battery improvements — the biggest long-term risk is Apple launching a true endurance-focused watch with multi-day GPS battery life. Watch closely for any major battery breakthrough.
  • Auto OEM and the Mercedes ramp — a 2026 decline is guided and expected, not a thesis breaker. What I'm watching for is confirmation the Mercedes-Benz program is on track to drive growth from 2027, since that's the concrete catalyst underpinning the segment's recovery.
  • Aviation segment growth — should run 5–8% annually, though it grew 18% in Q1 2026. Growth below 3% may signal slowing aircraft demand; sustained double-digit growth could mean FAA upgrade demand is stronger than expected.
