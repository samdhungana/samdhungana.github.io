I recommend longing Talen Energy (TLN), an independent power producer with roughly 15.6 GW of nuclear and gas capacity in PJM, because it is undervalued by 20 to 25 percent, and its stock price could increase significantly over the next six to twelve months. The stock rallied from $327 to a peak of $438 over the past four months, then pulled back to around $385 on a valuation-driven downgrade from Jefferies and an unexplained one-day drop on July 1. The market is treating this pullback as the end of the rally, when it is really just digesting a separate, still-unresolved PJM regulatory question rather than the AWS ramp or the Cornerstone acquisition. I believe the company's intrinsic value is close to $460 to $470 per share in my Base Case, and even in my Downside Case, where regulatory friction and slower permitting delay but do not derail the growth plan, the company is only overvalued by about 7 percent.

The Business

Talen Energy is an independent power producer centered on the Susquehanna nuclear plant in Pennsylvania, with a growing gas fleet across PJM. The company emerged from Chapter 11 in May 2023 and is the counterparty to a landmark power supply agreement with Amazon Web Services covering up to 1,920 MW through 2042.

In its most recent fiscal year, Talen generated $2.58 billion of revenue and $1.04 billion of Adjusted EBITDA, the measure the company and the Street both use, which strips out large one-time items like nuclear trust losses and impairments. My own DCF model builds EBITDA mechanically from GAAP sales, cost of goods sold, and SG&A, so its historical EBITDA line for that same year runs much lower, around $305 million, and the two figures should not be read as contradicting one another.

Its current market cap is $17.5 billion and its enterprise value is $23.3 billion, or 11.0 times 2026 EBITDA. I project a Base Case revenue CAGR of roughly 19 percent over the next five years, driven by the Cornerstone acquisition of 2,451 MW for $3.45 billion, closed in June 2026, and the AWS ramp, compared with a peer group median forward growth of roughly 7 percent.

Why This Is Mispriced

The market currently views Talen as a story that has already played out, and one still exposed to significant downside from PJM's capacity market redesign. I think each part of that view is incorrect.

First, the Jefferies downgrade was valuation driven, not fundamentals driven. Jefferies raised its price target to $453 the same day it cut its rating to Hold, and Goldman Sachs, with a $499 target, and BNP Paribas, with a $548 target, both carry price targets well above the current price.

Second, the market still associates PJM's Reliability Backstop Procurement with the sudden 7.5 percent selloff that followed its January 2026 proposal, but the mechanism as designed is a supplemental bilateral process that carves out existing contracts. FERC separately extended the existing price collar through two more auction years. Both are constructive outcomes, not compressive ones.

Third, consensus likely still underestimates how much of Talen's growth is already contractually locked in. PJM capacity prices for the 2026–2027 and 2027–2028 delivery years are already cleared at $329 to $333 per megawatt day, up from $29 to $34 before 2025, and Cornerstone's full-year contribution has not yet appeared in a complete fiscal year.

The AWS ramp and Cornerstone contribution together support roughly 15 percent of the implied undervaluation, and the market's overly cautious regulatory discount, once it unwinds, could add another 5 to 10 percent. Together, these imply the company is 20 to 25 percent undervalued.

Valuation

I valued Talen with a five-year discounted cash flow model covering 2026 through 2030, using the mid-year convention, a discount rate of 7.96 percent based on a target capital structure of 27.3 percent debt and 72.7 percent equity plus a peer beta analysis, and an exit multiple of 9.0 times terminal-year 2030 EBITDA, itself below the peer group's own forward multiple.

In my Base Case, revenue growth runs at 71.6 percent, 15.4 percent, 6.0 percent, 7.1 percent, and 4.1 percent from 2026 through 2030, sourced directly from FactSet consensus. COGS declines from 31.2 percent to 26.9 percent of sales and SG&A falls from 21.5 percent to 18.1 percent, as the mix shifts toward high-margin contracted capacity revenue. Capex runs at 6.7 percent, then 5.6 percent, then 6.5 percent of sales for the remaining years, based on the company's own sustaining capex guidance, and the marginal tax rate is 23.4 percent, made up of 21.0 percent federal and 2.4 percent state. Together these produce an implied share price of $467, about 21 percent above where the stock trades today.

My Upside Case assumes lower cost ratios alongside faster growth, reflecting an accelerated AWS ramp, further acquisitions, and a constructive outcome on the PJM regulatory process, and implies a share price of $623. My first Downside Case reflects regulatory friction and permitting delays, implying $359, about 7 percent below today's price. My second, more severe Downside Case reflects a shock to data center demand durability, implying $245, about 37 percent below today's price.

On a comparable-companies basis, Talen trades at 11.0 times its 2026 EBITDA, versus a peer median of 12.6 times, despite a Base Case growth rate roughly three times the peer average. This implies the market has not yet fully priced in the company's growth.

Catalysts

Cornerstone closed in June 2026, so 2027 will be the first full year its plants contribute. Management has guided to this adding meaningfully to free cash flow per share.

The AWS power agreement's front-of-meter transition was completed on schedule in April 2026, and the next visible milestone is 480 MW of contracted volume by 2028, on the way to the full 1,920 MW by 2032.

PJM's Reliability Backstop Procurement begins its bilateral matchmaking phase in September 2026. A calm rollout, consistent with the April 2026 design, would remove one of the last remaining overhangs on the stock.

Risks

If PJM's final Reliability Backstop Procurement design caps capacity prices more aggressively than expected, the implied share price could decline toward my first Downside Case of $359, about 7 percent below the current price.

If data center demand growth proves overstated, echoing PJM's own reduced summer 2027 peak forecast and ERCOT's reconsideration of approved Texas interconnections, the implied share price could fall further, toward my second Downside Case of $245, about 37 percent below the current price.

Local permitting friction, similar to the Montour County zoning denial the company faced in February 2026, is a smaller and more contained risk. Management has stated it does not affect the timing of contracted AWS power deliveries.

In the worst case, if all of the above happen together, the stock could fall further than my second Downside Case suggests, but this would require several distinct negative outcomes at once. I'd hedge against this by purchasing put options, longing other power producers levered to data center demand in markets less concentrated in PJM, or shorting a broader utility and independent power producer index.

Bottom Line

The Jefferies downgrade, the PJM regulatory overhang, and the pullback from $438 all read like the end of the story. I think they're a market still digesting an unresolved regulatory question rather than the AWS ramp or the Cornerstone acquisition, both of which are already contractually locked in and not yet fully reflected in a complete fiscal year. A $467 Base Case, sell-side targets clustered well above today's price, and a Downside Case that only implies 7 percent of overvaluation all point the same direction: TLN is mispriced on a fixable framing problem, not a broken thesis.
